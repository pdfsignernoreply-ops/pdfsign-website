Is Your Digital Signature Still Valid After the DSC Certificate Expires?

By PDFSign  ·  April 2026  ·  6 min read
Home › Blog › Digital Signature Valid After DSC Expiry

Your DSC certificate has a validity period — typically 1, 2, or 3 years. Once it expires, you renew it. But what happens to all the PDFs you signed with the old certificate? Are those signatures still legally valid in India?

The short answer: Yes — if the signature includes an RFC 3161 timestamp. Without a timestamp, the answer is more complicated.

The Core Legal Principle

Under the IT Act 2000 and the Information Technology (Certifying Authorities) Rules, 2000, what matters for a digital signature's legal validity is whether:

  1. The DSC certificate was valid at the time the document was signed
  2. The document has not been altered after signing
  3. The signature was created using the private key corresponding to the certified public key

Note that requirement #1 says "at the time of signing" — not "at the time of verification." This is the key insight. A certificate that has since expired does not retroactively invalidate signatures made while it was active.

Why a Timestamp Is Critical

The problem is proving when the signing occurred. Without evidence of the signing time, a verifier cannot determine whether the certificate was valid when the document was signed or whether it was signed after the certificate expired.

An RFC 3161 timestamp from a trusted Timestamp Authority (TSA) provides exactly this evidence. The TSA receives a hash of the signed document and issues a cryptographically signed timestamp token — a trusted, tamper-proof record of when the signing happened.

✅ With RFC 3161 Timestamp

  • Signing time is cryptographically proven
  • Signature valid forever (DSC may expire)
  • Adobe shows "Signature Valid" in green
  • Courts and portals can verify independently
  • Satisfies PAdES-T / PAdES-LT standard

❌ Without Timestamp

  • No proof of when signing occurred
  • After cert expiry: shows "validity unknown"
  • Cannot establish signing was within validity
  • Legally contestable in disputes
  • Basic PAdES-B only — not recommended

What Happens to the Signature After DSC Expiry?

Scenario 1 — Timestamped Signature (PAdES-T or LT)

When you open the PDF in Adobe Acrobat Reader after the DSC has expired:

  • The signature panel shows the signing date from the timestamp (not the current date)
  • Acrobat checks: was the certificate valid at the timestamp's recorded time? Yes → Signature Valid
  • The green checkmark and "Signed by [Name]" display correctly
  • The embedded OCSP data (in PAdES-LT) confirms the certificate had not been revoked at that time
This is the correct behavior. A timestamped signature produced while the DSC was valid will show as valid in Acrobat even 5 years after the DSC expired.

Scenario 2 — Signature Without Timestamp (PAdES-B)

If no timestamp was embedded at signing time:

  • After the certificate expires, Acrobat cannot determine when signing occurred
  • Acrobat may show "Signature validity is unknown" or a yellow warning
  • Some verifiers use the system clock as a fallback signing time, which creates a falsified "signing time" that is not cryptographically trustworthy
  • The signature is not necessarily invalid — but its validity is unproven
If your signing software does not embed an RFC 3161 timestamp, signed documents become difficult to verify after DSC expiry. Always confirm your signing tool embeds a timestamp before relying on signatures for long-term records.

What If the DSC Was Revoked (Not Just Expired)?

Expiry and revocation are different:

  • Expiry — certificate reaches its end-of-life date; signatures timestamped before expiry remain valid
  • Revocation — CA invalidates the certificate mid-life (e.g., private key compromised). Signatures made after the revocation date are invalid. Signatures made before the revocation date remain valid if timestamped before revocation.

The embedded OCSP data in a PAdES-LT signature records the revocation status at signing time, so the signature is protected even in revocation scenarios (as long as signing preceded the revocation).

Do I Need to Re-sign Documents After DSC Renewal?

No. Once a document is signed with a timestamped PAdES signature, it is permanently valid. Your DSC renewal creates a new certificate (with a new key pair), which you use for future signings. Previously signed documents are entirely independent of your new certificate.

Checking Whether Your Signed PDF Has a Timestamp

  1. Open the signed PDF in Adobe Acrobat Reader (free)
  2. Click the signature or open the signature panel (left sidebar)
  3. Click Signature Properties
  4. Look for Date/Time and check if it shows "from the signature's timestamp" or "from the clock on the signer's computer"
  5. If it says "from timestamp" — your signature is timestamped and will remain valid after expiry
  6. If it says "from clock" — the signature has no embedded timestamp

Frequently Asked Questions

I signed a tender document 2 years ago. My DSC expired last year. Is the signed document still valid for audit?

If PDFSign (or any PAdES-LT compliant tool) was used, yes. The RFC 3161 timestamp embedded at signing time proves the document was signed while your DSC was valid. Auditors and courts treat this as valid. If the signature had no timestamp, the validity is uncertain and should be reviewed by a legal professional.

The government portal accepted the signed file when I uploaded it. But will it still be readable after years?

Yes. The portal acceptance confirms authenticity at submission time. The embedded timestamp and OCSP data ensure the PDF remains verifiable independently of the portal. Store the signed PDF file securely for your records.

Does the CA matter — eMudhra, Capricorn, Vsign?

All CCA-licensed CAs (eMudhra, Capricorn, Vsign, SafeScrypt, etc.) issue certificates that chain to the CCA India root. As long as that root is trusted in Adobe's AATL and the Windows Certificate Store, signatures from any licensed CA verify correctly regardless of which CA you used.
