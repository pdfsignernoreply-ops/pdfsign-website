How to Verify a Digital Signature in a PDF — Complete Guide for India

By PDFSign.in  ·  Jan 2026  ·  5 min read
Home › Blog › How to Verify a Digital Signature in a PDF

You have received a digitally signed PDF — a contract, a tender document, a compliance certificate, or an audit report. Before relying on it or forwarding it to a government portal, you want to confirm that it is genuinely signed, has not been tampered with, and was signed by who it claims to be from.

This guide walks through exactly how to do that — using Adobe Acrobat Reader, browser-based viewers, and what to look for when a signature is valid versus suspicious.

What Is Inside a Digital Signature?

When someone signs a PDF using a USB DSC token and software like PDFSign.in, the resulting signature is not just a visual mark. It is a cryptographic data structure embedded in the PDF file that contains:

  • Signer's name and organisation — exactly as registered with the Certifying Authority (CA)
  • Certifying Authority — the licensed CA that issued the DSC (e.g., eMudhra, Capricorn, NSDL)
  • Certificate serial number — unique identifier for the specific DSC
  • Certificate validity period — issue date and expiry date of the DSC
  • Document hash — a cryptographic fingerprint of the document at the time of signing. If any byte in the document changes after signing, this hash will not match and the signature shows as invalid.
  • RFC 3161 timestamp — from a Timestamp Authority (TSA) server, proving the exact date and time of signing
  • OCSP revocation status — confirmation that the certificate had not been revoked at the time of signing

All of this is verifiable by anyone who opens the signed PDF in a PDF reader that supports digital signatures — without needing to contact the CA or any external server (assuming the signature includes LTV data).

Signature Status in PDF Viewers

When you open a signed PDF, the PDF reader shows one of these signature statuses:

✅Signature ValidFull chain verified. Document unmodified since signing.
⚠️Validity UnknownReader can't verify root cert locally. Signature may still be genuine.
❌Signature InvalidDocument modified after signing, or certificate revoked. Do not trust.

"Validity unknown" is a local trust configuration issue — not an indication of fraud. See our guide on fixing Signature Validity Unknown for the one-time fix. A red invalid status is more serious and should be investigated.

How to Verify in Adobe Acrobat Reader (Recommended)

Adobe Acrobat Reader is the most comprehensive free PDF reader for verifying digital signatures. Download the latest version from adobe.com if you do not have it installed.

1Open the Signed PDF in Adobe Acrobat Reader

Double-click the PDF file. If it does not open in Acrobat Reader automatically, right-click the file and choose Open With → Adobe Acrobat Reader.

2Look for the Signature Panel or Signature Field

At the top of the document, Adobe shows a blue or green information bar saying "Signed and all signatures are valid" or a similar message. You may also see a visible signature box on the page with the signer's name.

3Open the Signatures Panel

Go to View → Show/Hide → Navigation Panels → Signatures. Or click the Signatures icon in the left panel (looks like a pen nib). The Signatures panel lists all signatures in the document.

4Click on the Signature Entry

Click on a signature in the panel to expand its details. You will see the signature status (valid/unknown/invalid), the signer's name, and the time of signing.

5Click "Signature Properties" for Full Details

Right-click the signature entry and choose "Show Signature Properties" (or click the link in the signature panel). A detailed dialog opens showing the complete certificate information.

6Click "Show Certificate" for Signer Identity

In the Signature Properties dialog, click Show Certificate. This opens the certificate viewer showing the full certificate chain: your signer's certificate → CA's intermediate certificate → CCA India root certificate.

7Verify Key Information

In the certificate viewer, confirm: the signer's Common Name (their name) and Organisation match who you expect. The Issuer should be an Indian licensed CA (eMudhra, Capricorn, NSDL, SafeScrypt, (n)Code). The Validity period shows the DSC was valid at the time of signing.

Verifying in Chrome or Edge (Browser PDF Viewer)

Chrome and Edge can display PDFs and show basic digital signature information, but they do not fully verify the certificate chain. Limitations in browser-based verification:

  • Chrome and Edge may show "This document is signed" but cannot display the full certificate details
  • They cannot verify the CCA India root certificate chain in most configurations
  • They do not show the RFC 3161 timestamp details
  • They cannot distinguish between "validity unknown" and a genuine trust failure
For legally important documents — tender bids, audit reports, property agreements, legal notices — always use Adobe Acrobat Reader for signature verification, not a browser PDF viewer. Browser viewers are sufficient only for casual review of signed documents.

Red Flags: When to Question a Signature

Even if a signature shows as valid in Adobe, there are additional checks worth doing for high-stakes documents:

Unexpected Signer Name

The name in the certificate does not match the person who claims to have signed the document. Confirm with the sender if there is any discrepancy.

Certifying Authority Is Unknown

The issuer is not one of the licensed Indian CAs (eMudhra, Capricorn, NSDL, SafeScrypt, (n)Code, or others licensed by CCA India). An unknown or foreign CA signing a document that should have an Indian DSC is suspicious.

Certificate Was Already Expired at Time of Signing

Check the certificate validity dates versus the signing time. If the "valid to" date is before the signing date, and there is no RFC 3161 timestamp to establish when signing actually occurred, be cautious.

No Timestamp

A signature without an RFC 3161 timestamp cannot definitively prove when the document was signed. After the DSC expires, anyone reviewing the document in the future cannot determine if signing happened during validity. Always prefer documents signed with timestamps.

Signature Is Invalid (Red Cross)

A red invalid status is a hard stop. Either the document was tampered with after signing, or the certificate was revoked. Do not accept the document — request a fresh signed copy from the signatory.

When in doubt, ask for a re-sign. If you cannot verify a signature satisfactorily, ask the sender to re-sign the document with a fresh signature. A properly signed document takes under a minute to produce.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I verify a digital signature without Adobe Acrobat?

Chrome and Edge can display basic signature information, but for complete verification — especially confirming the CCA India certificate chain, timestamp, and revocation status — use Adobe Acrobat Reader with an updated trust list.

Is the signature still valid if the DSC has expired?

Yes, if signing occurred before expiry and an RFC 3161 timestamp was embedded. The timestamp is the legal proof of when signing occurred. If the timestamp confirms signing during the certificate's validity period, the signature remains legally valid after the DSC expires.

What does a red "Signature Invalid" mean?

Either the document was modified after signing (the cryptographic hash doesn't match) or the signing certificate was revoked. Either way, the signature cannot be trusted. Request a fresh signed document.

How do I know who signed the document?

Click the signature in Adobe Acrobat → Signature Properties → Show Certificate. The Common Name field shows the signer's name. The Organisation field shows their company (for org DSC). The Issuer field shows which CA issued the certificate.
