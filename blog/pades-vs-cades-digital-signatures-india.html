PAdES vs CAdES Digital Signatures — Which Standard Applies in India?

By PDFSign  ·  Apr 2026  ·  7 min read
Home › Blog › PAdES vs CAdES Digital Signatures

When your DSC token signs a PDF, it uses a specific cryptographic standard that determines how the signature is stored, how it appears in Adobe Acrobat, and whether it remains verifiable years later. The two most common standards are PAdES and CAdES — and knowing the difference matters if you file documents with Indian government portals or courts.

What Is PAdES?

PAdES (PDF Advanced Electronic Signatures) is an ETSI standard (EN 319 102) for embedding digital signatures directly inside a PDF file. When a PDF is signed with PAdES, the signature — including the certificate chain, timestamp, and OCSP/CRL data — is stored as part of the PDF document structure.

The result is a single .pdf file that:

  • Shows a blue or green signature panel in Adobe Acrobat Reader
  • Can be verified without any additional files or software
  • Supports visible signature fields (name, date, seal image)
  • Remains verifiable years after the DSC certificate expires

What Is CAdES?

CAdES (CMS Advanced Electronic Signatures) is an ETSI standard (EN 319 122) based on CMS (Cryptographic Message Syntax). It wraps a document with a cryptographic envelope and produces a separate .p7s or .p7m signature file.

CAdES is format-agnostic — it can sign any file type (PDF, Word, ZIP, XML). However, for PDFs specifically, it means:

  • Two files must stay together: the original PDF and the .p7s signature
  • Acrobat Reader cannot display CAdES signatures natively inside a PDF
  • Portals must specifically support CAdES verification

✅ PAdES — For PDF Signing

  • Signature embedded inside PDF
  • Single signed .pdf file output
  • Visible in Adobe Acrobat Reader
  • Timestamp & OCSP inside PDF
  • Accepted by all Indian govt portals
  • Standard for GeM, MCA21, CPPP, NIC

CAdES — For Generic File Signing

  • Signature stored in separate .p7s file
  • Two files needed to verify
  • Not natively readable in Acrobat
  • Used for email (S/MIME), code signing
  • Not suitable for PDF portals in India
  • Used in EU eIDAS for non-PDF workflows

PAdES Levels — From Basic to Archive

PAdES has four levels of signature completeness, each adding more data to ensure the signature can be verified long into the future:

PAdES-BBasic signature + certificate chain
PAdES-T+ RFC 3161 timestamp
PAdES-LT+ OCSP/CRL data embedded
PAdES-LTA+ Archive timestamp (decades)

PAdES-B (Basic)

Contains the cryptographic signature and the signer's certificate chain. No timestamp — so if the DSC certificate expires, the signing time cannot be proven. This is the minimum level; adequate for documents verified immediately but risky for long-term records.

PAdES-T (Timestamped)

Adds an RFC 3161 timestamp from a trusted TSA server at the moment of signing. The timestamp proves when the signing occurred, independently of certificate validity. If the DSC was valid at signing time, the signature remains valid even after the certificate expires.

PAdES-LT (Long-Term)

Embeds OCSP responses or CRL data so that the certificate status at signing time is captured inside the PDF. This allows offline verification without needing to contact the CA's servers years later — critical for tender records and legal documents.

PDFSign produces PAdES-LT signatures — the most practical level for Indian government and commercial use.

PAdES-LTA (Long-Term with Archive Timestamp)

Adds a second timestamp over the entire signature including the embedded OCSP data. This is used for archival scenarios — court records, land registry documents — where signatures must survive 20+ years and outlive both the TSA's and CA's own hash algorithms.

For most Indian business use cases — tender bids, MCA filings, GST exports, NIC eProcurement — PAdES-LT is the right level. It gives you timestamp-backed validity and offline verifiability without the complexity of archive timestamps.

XAdES — A Third Standard Worth Knowing

There is also XAdES (XML Advanced Electronic Signatures), used for signing XML documents. In India, XAdES appears in certain banking and NSDL workflows where the transaction data is XML-structured. For everyday PDF signing, XAdES is not relevant.

PDFSign contacts a trusted Timestamp Authority (TSA) and embeds a cryptographic timestamp at the moment of signing. This proves the exact signing time independently of the certificate's validity period.

The certificate revocation status is fetched from the CA's OCSP server and embedded into the PDF. This is the LT (Long-Term) component — it allows offline verification years later without contacting any server.

Verifying a PAdES Signature in India

Any recipient can verify a PAdES-LT signature using Adobe Acrobat Reader (free) without installing any plugin:

  1. Open the signed PDF in Adobe Acrobat Reader
  2. Click the signature panel or the blue signature badge
  3. Acrobat displays: signer name, certificate issuer (eMudhra / Capricorn / etc.), signing date/time from timestamp, and validity status
  4. If the CCA India root is trusted, status shows Signature Valid in green
Offline verification works because the OCSP data is embedded inside the PDF. No internet connection is needed to verify a PAdES-LT signed document.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I convert a CAdES signature to PAdES?

No — they are fundamentally different signature structures. If a document was signed with CAdES (producing a .p7s file), you cannot convert it to a PDF-embedded PAdES signature. You would need to re-sign the document with a PAdES-compliant tool.

Does the Indian IT Act 2000 specify PAdES?

The IT Act 2000 and the 2015 Amendment define electronic signatures and DSC requirements but do not mandate a specific signing format. However, CCA India's licensed CAs and portal operators have converged on PAdES for PDF documents as the de facto standard.

Will PAdES signatures survive a DSC certificate expiry?

Yes — if the signature includes an RFC 3161 timestamp (PAdES-T or higher). The timestamp proves the certificate was valid at the time of signing, which is all that legally matters. See our detailed guide: Digital Signature Validity After DSC Certificate Expiry.
