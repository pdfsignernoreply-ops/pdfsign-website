Digital Signature Shows "Validity Unknown" in PDF — How to Fix It

By PDFSign.in  ·  April 2026  ·  6 min read
Home › Blog › Digital Signature Validity Unknown Fix

You open a digitally signed PDF in Adobe Acrobat and see a yellow warning banner: "Signature validity is unknown." The signature panel shows a question mark instead of a green tick. Your client is worried. Your auditor is questioning the document.

Here is the critical truth: this is almost never a problem with the signature itself. In the vast majority of cases, the document is perfectly and legally signed. The issue lies in how your PDF viewer handles India's certificate authority chain — and it has a simple fix.

What "Validity Unknown" Actually Means

A digital signature in a PDF is backed by a certificate issued by a Certifying Authority (CA) — eMudhra, Capricorn, NSDL, SafeScrypt, or (n)Code. These CAs operate under the Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA), which is the apex body under the Government of India's IT Act 2000.

For Adobe Acrobat to display a green "Signature Valid" tick, it must trust the entire certificate chain: Your Certificate → Your CA's Intermediate Certificate → CCA India Root Certificate. Adobe maintains a global list of trusted roots called the Adobe Approved Trust List (AATL). The problem: CCA India's root certificates are not always pre-loaded in every version of Adobe Reader or Acrobat, especially on machines that have not been updated recently.

When Adobe cannot find the CCA India root in its trusted list, it shows "validity unknown" — not "invalid." There is a fundamental difference between these three states:

✅
Valid (green tick)Adobe verified the full certificate chain to a trusted root. Document is unmodified.
⚠️
Validity Unknown (amber ?)Adobe cannot verify the chain locally. Root cert not in trusted list. Signature may still be genuine.
❌
Invalid (red cross)Document was modified after signing, or the signer's certificate has been revoked. Investigate immediately.

Amber means "I cannot verify this right now" — not "this is fake." Red means "something is definitely wrong." If you see amber, apply the fixes below. If you see red, do not accept the document.

Why This Happens Specifically for Indian DSC Signatures

Indian Certifying Authorities (eMudhra, Capricorn, NSDL, SafeScrypt, (n)Code) issue certificates under the CCA India root certificate hierarchy. CCA India does participate in Adobe's AATL programme, but inclusion is not guaranteed for all root versions, and older Adobe installations may not have the latest trust list.

Additionally, many users in India use Adobe Reader (the free version), which may not auto-update its AATL in enterprise or restricted network environments. The result: perfectly valid Indian DSC signatures appearing as "unknown" to both the signer and the recipient.

Fix 1 — Update Adobe's Trusted List (Recommended)

This is the simplest fix and works for most users. Adobe can download the latest trusted root certificates directly:

1Open Adobe Acrobat or Reader

Ensure you are on version 2020 or newer. Earlier versions have limited AATL support.

2Go to Edit → Preferences (Ctrl+K on Windows)

On Mac: Acrobat → Preferences → Signatures.

3Click on "Trust Manager" in the left panel

You will see the Adobe Approved Trust List section at the top.

4Click "Update Now"

Adobe downloads the latest trusted root certificates from Adobe's servers. This requires an internet connection and takes about 30 seconds.

5Close and reopen your signed PDF

The signature should now show a green valid tick for DSC-signed documents from CCA-licensed CAs.

After updating the AATL, most Indian DSC signatures will immediately show as valid. This is a one-time fix — Adobe keeps the list updated automatically going forward.

Fix 2 — Manually Add CCA India Root Certificate

If the automatic update does not resolve the issue (restricted network, older Adobe version, or offline environment), you can manually import the CCA India root certificate:

1Download CCA India Root Certificate

Visit cca.gov.in → Repository → Root Certificates. Download the CCA India 2022 root certificate (.cer file).

2Open Acrobat → Edit → Preferences → Signatures

Under "Identities and Trusted Certificates", click "More."

3Click "Trusted Certificates" → Import

Browse and select the downloaded .cer file.

4Set as Trusted Root

In the import dialog, check "Use this certificate as a trusted root." Click OK.

5Reopen the PDF

Signatures from all CCA-licensed CAs — eMudhra, Capricorn, NSDL, SafeScrypt — will now verify with a green valid status.

Fix 3 — Windows Certificate Store (Advanced)

On Windows systems, you can also import the CCA root into the Windows Certificate Store and configure Adobe to use it:

  • Double-click the downloaded CCA .cer file → Install Certificate
  • Select "Local Machine" → Place in "Trusted Root Certification Authorities"
  • In Adobe: Edit → Preferences → Trust Manager → check "Load trusted root certificates from Windows Certificate Store"
  • Restart Adobe and reopen the PDF

What About Government Portals?

If you are submitting signed documents to GeM, CPPP, MCA21, NIC e-Procurement, or the Income Tax portal, the portal validates signatures server-side — it does not rely on what Adobe displays on your screen. A document showing "validity unknown" locally is still accepted and validated correctly by these portals.

If a government portal explicitly rejects your signed document, the reason is almost never "validity unknown." More likely causes: incorrect signature type (digital vs image), wrong PDF version, or the document was modified after signing. Contact the portal's helpdesk with the exact error message.

How PDFSign Ensures Maximum Long-Term Verifiability

When you sign a PDF using PDFSign, the software applies a PAdES (PDF Advanced Electronic Signatures) compliant signature and embeds three layers of verification data:

  • Full Certificate Chain — your DSC certificate plus the issuing CA's intermediate certificate, embedded inside the PDF. Recipients do not need to download anything separately.
  • RFC 3161 Trusted Timestamp — from a Timestamp Authority (TSA) server, cryptographically proving the exact date and time of signing. This is separate from your DSC and remains valid independently.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is my digitally signed document legally valid even if Adobe shows validity unknown?

Yes, absolutely. Legal validity under IT Act 2000 is determined by whether the signature was applied using a valid DSC from a CCA-licensed CA during the certificate's validity period — not by what Adobe displays. The amber status is a client-side display limitation, not a legal finding.

Does the person receiving my signed PDF need to do anything?

For government portals (GeM, CPPP, MCA21), no action is needed from either party — portals validate server-side. For individual recipients (clients, lawyers, banks), ask them to do Edit → Preferences → Trust Manager → Update Now in their Adobe. This is a one-time fix that solves the issue for all future Indian-signed documents.

My colleague's signed documents show green but mine show amber. Why?

Your Adobe installation has an older trust list that does not include the latest CCA India root. Update the AATL as described in Fix 1 above. After updating, your documents will show correctly too.

I signed with PDFSign and the output still shows unknown. Is PDFSign the problem?

No. PDFSign embeds the full certificate chain in every signature. The amber display happens on the recipient's Adobe installation because their local trust list is out of date. The fix is always on the viewer's side, not on the signing side. Share this article with them.
